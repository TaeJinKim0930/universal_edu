## Problem Statement
Korean older adults, especially retired adults who are uncomfortable with digital financial workflows, receive confusing bank or card messages and freeze. They do not know whether the message is dangerous, urgent, or routine, and they risk either panicking and taking the wrong action or ignoring something important.
The hackathon framing is AI education, but the product should teach through action. Instead of abstract lessons about AI, the agent helps the user through a real-life financial moment, explains what is happening in plain Korean, and prepares the user safely before they act.
## What Makes This Cool
This feels like AI education without feeling like school.
The user uploads a real scary message, and the app turns that anxiety into a calm, concrete preparation plan. The magic is not “the AI answered me.” The magic is “it stopped me from doing something dumb, and now I know what to do next.”
## Constraints
- Must fit an AI education hackathon narrative.
- Must not feel like a generic chatbot or LMS.
- Must feel agentic, but not creepy or invasive.
- Must be privacy-safe, with user-triggered or clearly opt-in proactive behavior.
- Should work for Korean adults age 40-70, with the demo optimized for retired older adults.
- Must be understandable in a high-anxiety moment, so the UX should be direct, low-load, and one step at a time.
## Premises
1. The real problem is panic and unsafe action after confusing financial messages, not general digital illiteracy.
2. The MVP wedge is upload screenshot → calm explanation → visible risk reasons → safe action plan → optional reminder.
3. “Agentic” should mean preparing the user before action, not autonomous financial actions or hidden surveillance.
4. The strongest hackathon story is “AI education through real-life guided action,” not classroom-style education content.
## Approaches Considered
### Approach A: Guided Safety Coach
User uploads a bank/card message, the app explains it in plain Korean, shows visible risk reasons, then gives a safe action plan and optional reminder.
- Effort: S
- Risk: Low
- Pros:
  - Fastest to build and easiest to demo
  - Very clear before/after story
  - Strong educational value through real examples
- Cons:
  - Can feel like a smart explainer instead of a true agent
  - Weakens the proactive feel if the reminder is thin
### Approach B: Pre-Action Education Agent
User uploads the message, then the agent builds a short “prepare before acting” flow: what this means, what to avoid, what to do next, what to bring or check later, plus a follow-up reminder.
- Effort: M
- Risk: Low-Med
- Pros:
  - Best balance of trust, usefulness, and agentic feel
  - Feels proactive without crossing into creepy automation
  - Strongest match to the desired reaction: “it prepares me before I act”
- Cons:
  - Requires tighter UX sequencing
  - Slightly harder than a pure explainer to demo crisply
### Approach C: AI First Step Learning Companion
Wrap the same message flow inside a broader “AI 첫걸음” education product with mini lessons, confidence scoring, and repeat-use learning history.
- Effort: M-L
- Risk: High
- Pros:
  - Sounds more conventionally educational on paper
  - Broader long-term vision
- Cons:
  - Much weaker hackathon demo
  - Risks becoming generic and diluted
  - Pulls attention away from the emotional core user moment
## Recommended Approach
Choose Approach B, the Pre-Action Education Agent.
This is the sharpest version because it preserves the AI education framing while making the product feel like a real agent. The assistant should not just classify the message. It should reduce panic, explain the situation in plain Korean, show why it is risky or normal, and prepare the user for the next safe step.
The core demo loop:
1. User uploads a screenshot of a confusing bank or card message.
2. The app immediately shows a calm “pause before action” screen.
3. The agent explains the message in plain Korean.
4. The agent shows visible reasons for the risk assessment, for example urgency language, suspicious link, OTP request, or mismatch with typical bank behavior.
5. The agent gives a safe action plan, such as open the official app, call the number on the back of the card, or prepare what to say to a bank agent.
6. The app offers an opt-in reminder to follow up before the user acts or later that day.

## Success Criteria
- A judge can understand the product in under 20 seconds.
- The demo clearly shows one scary message becoming one calm, safe plan.
- The product feels more like a protective guide than a chatbot.
- The educational value is obvious through the flow itself, without adding lesson modules.
- The proactive reminder feels helpful and consent-based, not invasive.
